What is a Heart Attack?

A heart attack, medically known as a myocardial infarction, occurs when blood flow to the heart muscle is severely reduced or completely blocked. This blockage prevents oxygen and nutrients from reaching the heart tissue, causing cells to begin dying within minutes.

The Science Behind a Heart Attack

Most heart attacks are caused by coronary artery disease (CAD), where the coronary arteries that supply blood to the heart become narrowed due to a buildup of plaque‚Äîa combination of fat, cholesterol, and other substances.

When plaque ruptures, a blood clot forms around it. If this clot becomes large enough, it can block the flow of blood through the artery, leading to a heart attack.

Silent Heart Attack

Some heart attacks don't cause obvious symptoms, especially in people with diabetes. These "silent" heart attacks can still cause significant damage.

MINOCA

Myocardial infarction with non-obstructive coronary arteries (MINOCA) occurs when heart attacks happen without major blockages in arteries.

Heart Attack Symptoms and Warning Signs

Recognizing the symptoms of a heart attack is crucial for getting prompt treatment. Symptoms can vary between men and women and may develop suddenly or gradually over hours, days, or even weeks.

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: Don't Ignore These Signs

If you experience any of these symptoms, especially if they're severe or last more than 5 minutes, seek emergency medical help immediately.

Common Symptoms

  • Chest pain or discomfort (pressure, squeezing, fullness)
  • Pain in arms, back, neck, jaw, or stomach
  • Shortness of breath
  • Cold sweat
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Lightheadedness or dizziness

Symptoms More Common in Women

  • Unusual fatigue
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Anxiety
  • Indigestion or gas-like pain
  • Upper back, shoulder, or throat pain

  • Face: Does one side of the face droop?
  • Arms: Is one arm weak or numb?
  • Speech: Is speech slurred or strange?
  • Time: If you observe these signs, call emergency services immediately

Common Causes and Risk Factors

Understanding what causes heart attacks and the factors that increase your risk is the first step toward prevention. Some risk factors are within your control, while others are not.

Controllable Risk Factors

  • Smoking: Damages blood vessels and increases heart disease risk
  • High blood pressure: Forces your heart to work harder
  • High cholesterol: Leads to plaque buildup in arteries
  • Diabetes: Increases risk, especially if poorly controlled
  • Obesity: Linked to higher cholesterol and blood pressure
  • Physical inactivity: Weakens the heart and circulatory system
  • Unhealthy diet: High in saturated fats, trans fats, and cholesterol
  • Stress: May raise blood pressure and damage arteries

Uncontrollable Risk Factors

  • Age: Risk increases for men over 45 and women over 55
  • Family history: Higher risk if close relatives had early heart disease
  • Gender: Men have greater risk, though women's risk increases after menopause
  • Race: African Americans, Mexican Americans, and Native Americans have higher risks

Emerging Risk Factors

Research continues to identify new factors that may contribute to heart attack risk:

  • Sleep apnea: Disrupted breathing during sleep strains the cardiovascular system
  • High-sensitivity C-reactive protein: A marker for inflammation in the body
  • High triglycerides: A type of fat in the blood
  • Preeclampsia: A condition during pregnancy that increases lifelong risk

The good news is that up to 80% of premature heart attacks and strokes are preventable. Lifestyle changes and, when necessary, medications can dramatically reduce your risk.

The 5 Numbers That Matter Most

Keep these five numbers in a healthy range to significantly reduce your heart attack risk:

  1. Total cholesterol: Less than 200 mg/dL
  2. Blood pressure: Less than 120/80 mm Hg
  3. Fasting blood sugar: Less than 100 mg/dL
  4. Body mass index (BMI): 18.5 to 24.9
  5. Waist circumference: Less than 35 inches for women, 40 inches for men

What to Do in a Heart Attack Emergency

üö® Immediate Action Saves Lives

If you think you or someone else is having a heart attack, call emergency services immediately. Don't wait to see if symptoms go away. Every minute matters.

Step-by-Step Emergency Response

1

Call Emergency Services

Don't drive yourself to the hospital. Emergency medical personnel can begin treatment immediately and provide faster care at the hospital.

2

Chew Aspirin

If not allergic, chew one regular-strength aspirin (325 mg) or four low-dose aspirin (81 mg each). Aspirin helps prevent blood clotting.

3

Take Nitroglycerin

If prescribed, take nitroglycerin as directed while waiting for emergency help.

4

Begin CPR if Needed

If the person is unconscious and not breathing, begin hands-only CPR (chest compressions at 100-120 per minute) until help arrives or an AED is available.

AEDs Save Lives

Automated external defibrillators (AEDs) are available in many public places. These devices can analyze heart rhythm and deliver an electric shock if needed to restore normal rhythm. They provide simple audio instructions for use.

Recovery and Life After a Heart Attack

Recovery from a heart attack takes time, both physically and emotionally. With proper care and lifestyle changes, many people return to normal activities and enjoy a good quality of life.

Cardiac Rehabilitation

Cardiac rehab is a supervised program that includes:

  • Physical activity guidance
  • Education about heart-healthy living
  • Counseling to reduce stress and improve mental health

Participation in cardiac rehab can reduce the risk of future heart problems and improve recovery.

Medications

After a heart attack, most people need medications such as:

  • Blood thinners (antiplatelet drugs)
  • Beta blockers
  • ACE inhibitors
  • Statins (cholesterol-lowering drugs)

Take all medications as prescribed and don't stop without consulting your doctor.

Emotional Recovery

It's common to experience depression, anxiety, or fear after a heart attack. These feelings are normal but should be addressed. Consider:

  • Joining a support group
  • Speaking with a mental health professional
  • Sharing your feelings with loved ones
  • Practicing stress-reduction techniques

Your Heart Health Journey

Understanding heart attacks is the first step toward prevention and effective response. By knowing the risk factors, symptoms, and prevention strategies, you're taking control of your heart health.

Remember, small lifestyle changes can make a big difference. Start with one change today, whether it's taking a walk, choosing a heart-healthy meal, or scheduling a check-up with your doctor.
